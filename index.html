1
Inputs
Spread the table — no analysis yet
List everything you're bringing to the table: articles, interviews, field notes, policy documents, lived experience stories, data sets, images. Don't interpret. Just catalogue what's here.
2
Observations
I notice… — stay close to the evidence
Read across your inputs independently. Write "I notice…" statements — one per row. No interpretation, no analysis. Just what you see at the surface. Keep going until you have at least 8–10.
3
Themes
Group & name as tensions, not topics
Cluster your observations into groups. Name each cluster as a tension or dynamic, not a topic label. Aim for 3–6 themes. Example: instead of "Trust" try "Trust erodes faster than it builds."
4
Insights
We thought… but actually… — the reframe
An insight is not a theme. It's a surprising reframe of what you thought you knew. Use the format: [Who] needs [what] because [unexpected truth]. Aim for 3–5 strong insights.
5
Problem Statements
POV Madlib — human-centred, not solution-led
Use the POV Madlib: [User] needs [verb] because [compelling insight]. One per insight. These are your bridge into HMW — they should be specific enough to focus energy, broad enough to avoid prescribing solutions.

How Might We
Open the space — solution-neutral, positively framed
Reframe each problem statement starting with "How might we…" Then check: could 10 teams answer this 10 different ways? If yes — strong. If it points to one solution — too narrow. If it could be anything — too broad.

Worked Example

Inclusive AI — Omidyar
Stage 1
Inputs
Omidyar Network: Inclusive AI framework documents
Field interviews with frontline social workers using automated case management tools
Academic papers on algorithmic bias in welfare systems
Community forum transcripts from affected neighbourhoods
Policy documents: EU AI Act, US Algorithmic Accountability Act
News articles on AI rollout failures in public services
Stage 2
Observations
I notice most AI ethics frameworks centre English-speaking, formally educated users
I notice community trust in AI systems varies significantly by prior institutional experience
I notice "inclusion" in most articles refers to dataset diversity, not decision-making power
I notice frontline workers are rarely consulted in AI procurement decisions
I notice harm travels faster through AI systems than accountability does
Stage 3
Themes
Representation in data ≠ representation in power
Participation is offered; control is not
Trust erodes faster than it is built
Opacity is a governance choice, not a technical necessity
Stage 4
Insights
Communities affected by AI systems need meaningful intervention points — not feedback channels — because consultation without consequence is a form of exclusion
Frontline practitioners need legible explanations of AI recommendations because opacity erodes trust faster than errors do
Marginalized groups need to be co-designers of AI governance, not subjects of impact assessments, because representation in data doesn't translate to representation in power
Stage 5
Problem Statements
Affected community members need genuine decision-making authority in AI governance processes because being consulted is not the same as being heard
Frontline social workers need clear explanations of how AI recommendations are made because they are accountable for decisions they don't fully understand
Marginalised groups need structured roles as co-designers of AI systems because their absence from the design table reproduces the harms they already face
Stage 6
HMWs
How might we design AI governance so that affected communities can shape outcomes — not just respond to them?
How might we make AI decision-making legible to those most impacted, without requiring technical fluency?
How might we shift co-design from a research method into a durable institutional practice?
How might we ensure accountability travels at the same speed as harm through AI systems?
Stage 7
Activities
Service safaris: experience AI-mediated services as community members do
Ecosystem mapping: visualise all actors, flows of data, decisions and accountability
Co-design sessions: frame as collaborative authorship, not user research
Experience prototyping: what does it feel like to be invited into AI governance?
Service blueprinting: map the backstage as carefully as the frontstage
