# Service Safari
## Embodied Knowing — Walk in the Organism's Niche

---

### Nature Analog
A naturalist doesn't study a jaguar from a laboratory. They enter the jaguar's territory, move at its pace, experience the heat, the smell, the obstacles. **Ethology** — the study of behaviour in natural environments — teaches us that you cannot understand a living system by observing it from outside its context.

> *"You cannot understand a system you are not part of."*
> — Gregory Bateson

---

### Biological Principle
**Embodied cognition.** Understanding emerges through the body moving through an environment, not through abstraction. Octopuses solve puzzles their arms have never encountered. Bees navigate without GPS. Their intelligence is distributed through their whole organism engaging with the world.

---

### What It Is
A Service Safari is structured fieldwork where you **become the user** — you experience the service, system, or ecosystem exactly as those inside it do. You follow the same path, encounter the same friction, wait in the same queues, use the same interfaces.

This is not mystery shopping. It is ethnographic immersion with a design lens.

---

### When to Use
- **Observe phase** — before you have formed opinions, to fill your field notebook with raw material
- **Adapt phase** — after prototyping, to test whether your concept works in the actual environment
- When your team is dominated by people who have never used the service they are designing

---

### Biomimetic Method

**Before the Safari**
1. Define the organism you are becoming — which user, which context, which moment in their journey
2. Set your sensory brief: what will you *feel*, *hear*, *wait for*, *not understand*?
3. Leave assumptions at the door — a naturalist does not enter the field with conclusions

**During the Safari**
1. Experience the service from the first contact point to the last
2. Document through sensation: *what confused me, what relieved me, what made me feel small, what felt dignified*
3. Photograph friction points — the worn path through the grass, not the paved one
4. Time everything — waiting is data
5. Talk to no one from your team while inside the experience

**After the Safari**
1. Write field notes immediately — within the hour
2. Use "I notice…" language — stay close to observation, resist interpretation
3. Mark moments of **energy change** — where did the system feel alive vs. dead?

---

### Outputs
- Field notebook of "I notice…" observations
- A map of **energy points** — where the experience peaks and collapses
- Raw material for your Pattern stage

---

### Facilitation Notes
- Run solo or in pairs — groups disrupt the ecosystem you are studying
- If you cannot enter the actual service, find the closest proxy environment
- Pay special attention to **edge conditions** — what happens at the margins of the system, not just its centre

---

### Time
**Preparation:** 30 min  
**Safari:** 1–3 hours (the full service journey)  
**Debrief:** 45 min

---

### Connected Stages
→ **Observe** (primary)  
→ **Adapt** (secondary — testing prototypes in-context)
