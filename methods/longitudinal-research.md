# Longitudinal Research
## Seasonal Rhythms — Some Patterns Only Emerge Through Time

---

### Nature Analog
A phenologist — a scientist who studies recurring seasonal events — understands that you cannot observe spring migration by visiting the marsh once. You return at the same time each year. You track the date the first swallow arrives, the first frog calls, the date the ice breaks. Meaning emerges from the **comparison across seasons**, not from any single observation.

> *"The land knows you, even when you have forgotten it."*
> — Robin Wall Kimmerer

---

### Biological Principle
**Temporal ecology.** Many ecological relationships are invisible except over time — species interactions, population dynamics, succession patterns. A single visit produces a snapshot. Return visits produce a story. The story is where understanding lives.

---

### What It Is
Longitudinal Research means **returning to the same people and places** at intervals to track how their experience of a system changes. It reveals what one-time research cannot: adaptation, drift, degradation, and emergence.

It answers the question: *does this system stay healthy through seasons?*

---

### When to Use
- **Observe phase** — to build a baseline you can compare against
- **Adapt phase** — to track whether your prototype produces durable change or just initial novelty
- **Propagate phase** — to understand whether what you have propagated survives its first winter
- When you suspect the current state is not the stable state — when the system is in flux

---

### Biomimetic Method

**Establish the Baseline**
On your first visit, document:
- The current state of key indicators (like a naturalist's phenology notebook)
- Who are the keystone individuals — the people whose experience will tell you most about system health?
- What are the markers you will track? Make them specific and observable.

**Design Your Return Rhythm**
Align your return visits with the **natural rhythms of the system**:
- After a season change (funding cycles, school years, harvest seasons)
- After a known stress event (winter, a policy change, a crisis)
- At the moment when a previous initiative would typically have degraded

Avoid: arbitrary intervals. Ask: *when would nature reveal something?*

**Return Visits: Track the Delta**
At each return visit:
1. Re-interview the same keystone individuals with the same opening questions
2. Ask: *"What has changed since we last spoke?"*
3. Document what is **the same, what has shifted, what has disappeared**
4. Note what has appeared that wasn't there before

**Read the Succession**
Over time, map the **succession pattern**:
- What grew from your early observations?
- What died back?
- What colonised the space that was cleared?
- Is the system moving toward greater health and complexity, or toward simplification and brittleness?

---

### Outputs
- A longitudinal record — field notes across seasons
- Delta maps: what changed and in which direction
- A succession story — the arc of the system's health over time
- Evidence for whether an intervention produced durable change

---

### Facilitation Notes
- The relationship you build over time is itself a form of service design — participants who trust you share more than those who meet you once
- Document the relationship itself as part of the research — longitudinal research changes the researcher too
- Small, consistent signals over time are more reliable than large signals from a single moment

---

### Time
**Per visit:** 1–2 hours  
**Return interval:** Monthly, quarterly, or seasonally depending on system rhythm  
**Minimum for meaningful longitudinal data:** 3 return visits

---

### Connected Stages
→ **Observe** (baseline)  
→ **Adapt** (primary — tracking whether change is durable)  
→ **Propagate** (secondary — tracking whether what you planted survived)
