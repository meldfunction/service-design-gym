# Ecosystem Mapping
## Network Topology — Making Invisible Flows Visible

---

### Nature Analog
An ecologist mapping a wetland doesn't just catalogue species. They trace **flows** — energy, nutrients, water, information — through the system. They map who eats whom, who shelters whom, who poisons whom. The map reveals the **architecture of dependency** that keeps the system alive or kills it.

> *"In nature, nothing exists alone."*
> — Rachel Carson

---

### Biological Principle
**Trophic cascades.** When wolves were reintroduced to Yellowstone, rivers changed course. One actor's presence restructured the entire ecosystem — because every actor is entangled with every other through chains of dependency. You cannot understand one element without mapping its relationships.

---

### What It Is
Ecosystem Mapping is a visual method for tracing **all the flows** between actors in a service system: resources, data, decisions, accountability, money, trust, and care. It makes visible what is usually invisible — the backstage architecture that sustains or undermines the frontstage experience.

Unlike a stakeholder map (which only shows actors), an ecosystem map shows **what moves between them**.

---

### When to Use
- **Pattern phase** — to surface the structural tensions your observations point toward
- **Emulate phase** — to understand which flows need redesigning before writing HMWs
- When the problem feels complex and multi-actor — when changing one thing affects everything else

---

### Biomimetic Method

**Step 1: Catalogue the Species**
List every actor in the ecosystem — users, providers, funders, regulators, informal networks, community actors, non-human actors (technology, infrastructure, environment). Include the actors who are absent but whose absence shapes the system.

**Step 2: Map the Flows**
Between each pair of connected actors, draw the flows. Use different colours or weights for:
- 🟢 **Resources** (money, material, capacity)
- 🔵 **Information** (data, decisions, signals)
- 🟡 **Trust** (reputation, permission, social capital)
- 🔴 **Accountability** (who is responsible to whom)
- ⚪ **Care** (emotional labour, informal support)

**Step 3: Find the Trophic Structure**
- Where do flows **accumulate**? (these are power concentrations)
- Where do flows **break down**? (these are failure points)
- Which actors are **keystone species** — whose removal would collapse the system?
- Which actors are **edge dwellers** — present but invisible, often doing critical work?

**Step 4: Name the Architecture**
Write 3–5 sentences that describe the structural logic of the system: *"This is a system where accountability flows upward but risk flows downward. Resources concentrate at the institutional centre while need concentrates at the community edge."*

---

### Outputs
- A visual ecosystem map (analogue or digital)
- A list of structural tensions — where flows misalign
- Named keystone actors whose change would reshape the whole system
- Raw material for Pattern themes and Emulate insights

---

### Facilitation Notes
- Do this with mixed actor groups — a map drawn only by designers misses the flows they can't see
- The most important flows are usually the ones no one is tracking
- Blank space on the map is data — who is absent from the ecosystem entirely?

---

### Time
**Solo or pair prep:** 45 min  
**Group mapping session:** 90 min  
**Sense-making:** 45 min

---

### Connected Stages
→ **Pattern** (primary)  
→ **Emulate** (secondary — identifying which flows to redesign)
