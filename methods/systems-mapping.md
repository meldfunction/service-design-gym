# Systems Mapping
## Whole-System Vision — Reading the Currents Your Design Swims In

---

### Nature Analog
A salmon doesn't fight the river randomly. It reads the current — the temperature gradients, the pressure differentials, the oxygen levels — and navigates accordingly. Understanding the systemic forces at work is what makes navigation possible. A designer who doesn't understand the system they are working in is swimming blind.

> *"You cannot change a complex system faster than it can adapt."*
> — Donella Meadows

---

### Biological Principle
**Adaptive cycles.** Panarchy theory describes how ecosystems move through growth (r), conservation (K), collapse (Ω), and reorganisation (α) phases. A system's position in this cycle determines what interventions are possible. Pushing for growth in a collapse phase is futile. Reorganisation phases are the windows of maximum leverage.

---

### What It Is
Systems Mapping is a method for making visible the **wider forces** — policies, incentive structures, power dynamics, cultural logics, historical legacies — that shape the system your design must navigate.

Unlike Ecosystem Mapping (which maps actors and flows), Systems Mapping zooms out to the **structural level**: the currents, not just the organisms in them.

---

### When to Use
- **Pattern phase** — to understand why the tensions you've named persist despite everyone's good intentions
- **Emulate phase** — to understand which structural levers your HMWs are (or should be) pulling
- **Propagate phase** — to understand what systemic conditions need to be true for your design to survive

---

### Biomimetic Method

**Level 1: Stock and Flow**
Map the key stocks (resources, capacities, relationships that accumulate) and flows (what increases or decreases them). Ask:
- What are the critical stocks in this system?
- What are the inflows and outflows?
- Where do stocks concentrate? Where do they deplete?

**Level 2: Feedback Loops**
Identify the feedback loops that reinforce or balance the system:
- 🔄 **Reinforcing loops** — the more X, the more X (vicious or virtuous cycles)
- ⚖️ **Balancing loops** — mechanisms that stabilise the system (often preserving the status quo)

Mark which loops are **currently dominant** — these are the forces your design is working against or with.

**Level 3: Leverage Points**
Donella Meadows' hierarchy of leverage points, adapted for biomimicry:

| Leverage Point | Nature Analog | Example |
|----------------|---------------|---------|
| Constants and parameters | Metabolic rate | Funding levels |
| Buffer sizes | Body fat reserves | Emergency capacity |
| Flow rates | Blood flow | Process speeds |
| Feedback loop strength | Immune response | Accountability mechanisms |
| **Information flows** | **Pheromone signals** | **Who gets what data** |
| **Rules and incentives** | **Evolutionary pressure** | **Policy and funding logic** |
| **System goals** | **Species' survival drive** | **What the institution optimises for** |
| **Paradigm** | **Evolutionary strategy** | **The story the system tells about itself** |

The higher up this list, the more powerful — and the harder to change.

**Level 4: Where in the Adaptive Cycle?**
Place the system on the adaptive cycle:
- **Growth (r)**: rapid expansion, high connectivity, low resilience
- **Conservation (K)**: stable, efficient, brittle, resistant to change
- **Collapse (Ω)**: system releasing — chaotic but full of potential
- **Reorganisation (α)**: seeds of new system forming

Most human services are stuck in **K** — stable, efficient, and incapable of systemic change. Collapse and reorganisation phases are where biomimicry strategies have the most purchase.

---

### Outputs
- A systems map showing stocks, flows, and feedback loops
- Named dominant loops — the forces you are working with or against
- Leverage point analysis — where is the highest-impact intervention?
- Adaptive cycle placement — what kind of change is currently possible?

---

### Facilitation Notes
- Run with a mixed group including people with institutional memory — they know which loops have been dominant longest
- Resist the urge to simplify. Complexity is not a presentation problem.
- A systems map is a hypothesis, not a fact — it should be updated as you learn

---

### Time
**Preparation:** 45 min  
**Mapping session:** 2–3 hours  
**Leverage point analysis:** 1 hour
