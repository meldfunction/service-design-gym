# Desk Research
## Horizon Scanning — What Has Evolution Already Solved?

---

### Nature Analog
Biomimicry begins in the library before it enters the field. Janine Benyus's foundational practice is to ask: *what organism has already solved this problem?* The answer usually exists — in the biological literature, in indigenous knowledge systems, in evolutionary history. **3.8 billion years of R&D is available for consultation.**

> *"The more our world functions like the natural world, the more likely we are to endure on this home planet."*
> — Janine Benyus

---

### Biological Principle
**Convergent evolution.** Unrelated species independently evolve the same solution to the same problem — dolphin echolocation and bat echolocation; the eye of an octopus and the eye of a vertebrate. This is nature telling you that certain designs are optimal for certain conditions. When you find convergence in your desk research, you have found a deep truth.

---

### What It Is
Desk Research is **active horizon scanning** — not passive reading but purposeful searching for analogues, precedents, and evidence that answers your HMWs.

In the biomimicry model, desk research has two parallel tracks:
1. **Human precedents** — what has been tried in service design, policy, and social systems
2. **Biological precedents** — what organisms and ecosystems have solved the analogous challenge

---

### When to Use
- **Observe phase** — to build the foundation of your field notebook
- **Emulate phase** — to find biological analogues for your insights
- **Propagate phase** — to find precedents for propagation mechanisms that have worked elsewhere
- When you have a strong HMW and want to understand what the evidence base already knows

---

### Biomimetic Method

**Track 1: Human Precedents**

Ask: *Where has a similar challenge been well-solved in human systems?*

Search across:
- Academic literature (Google Scholar, JSTOR)
- Practice documentation (IDEO, Nesta, Dark Matter Labs, Transition Design)
- Policy reports and evaluations
- Journalism and long-form narrative (often contains what academia misses)
- Indigenous knowledge systems (with appropriate attribution and humility)

Document: **what worked, what failed, and in what conditions**.

**Track 2: Biological Analogues**

Ask: *What organism or ecosystem has solved a version of this problem?*

The AskNature.org database (Biomimicry Institute) is the canonical starting point. Search by function, not by organism:
- *"How does nature distribute resources without central control?"* → Mycelium, ant colonies, river systems
- *"How does nature build trust between strangers?"* → Symbiosis, mutualism, honest signalling
- *"How does nature protect edges while maintaining exchange?"* → Mangrove roots, cell membranes, ecotones

For each biological analogue, extract:
- The principle (what strategy does the organism use?)
- The conditions (what environment does it work in?)
- The translation (what would this principle look like in a service system?)

**Synthesise: Convergent Solutions**
Where Track 1 and Track 2 point to the **same structural principle**, you have found convergent evidence. This is your strongest signal.

---

### Outputs
- An annotated evidence map: human precedents + biological analogues side by side
- A list of convergent solutions — where nature and human systems have independently found the same answer
- New HMW candidates that emerge from biological analogues
- A bibliography with direct links to sources

---

### Facilitation Notes
- Allocate equal time to biological and human precedent research — most teams over-invest in the human track
- AskNature.org, IBIS (Integrated Biodiversity Information Facility), and the Biomimicry 3.8 Toolbox are essential resources
- Document what you *don't* find — the absence of precedent is also data

---

### Time
**Focused session:** 2–4 hours  
**Deep review:** 1–2 days for complex problems  
**Synthesis:** 1 hour
