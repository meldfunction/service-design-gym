# Stakeholder Interviews
## Deep Listening — Reading the Organism's Internal State

---

### Nature Analog
A field ecologist studying wolf behaviour doesn't just observe movement patterns. They track hormonal signals, vocalisation, scat composition, sleep patterns. They are reading the **internal state** of the organism — the drives, fears, and needs that generate surface behaviour. What you see on the surface is an expression of something much deeper.

> *"Behaviour is the vocabulary. Motivation is the grammar."*

---

### Biological Principle
**Signals and receptors.** Every cell in a living system is broadcasting signals about its internal state. The art of biology is learning to read those signals — not just the gross behaviour, but the chemical, electrical, structural signals that reveal what the system is actually doing and why.

---

### What It Is
A Stakeholder Interview is a structured deep-listening practice. You are not gathering opinions. You are reading **the internal state** of a person — their motivations, constraints, mental models, fears, and the invisible rules they operate by.

The goal is not to confirm what you already think. It is to encounter the reality that exists outside your assumptions.

---

### When to Use
- **Observe phase** — to gather raw material from people inside the ecosystem
- **Pattern phase** — to deepen your understanding of why patterns exist
- **Adapt phase** — to understand what your prototype revealed about people's real mental models
- With non-user actors: staff, managers, regulators, community leaders — the species often overlooked

---

### Biomimetic Method

**Choose Your Organisms**
Interview across the full ecosystem:
- **Users** — the animal at the centre of your habitat study
- **Frontline practitioners** — the organisms doing the hidden maintenance work
- **Gatekeepers** — those who control access to resources or decisions
- **Edge dwellers** — those at the margins of the system, often with the sharpest view of its logic
- **Former users** — those who left the ecosystem; their exit is data

**Prepare: Enter Without a Map**
1. Write your assumptions before the interview — not to validate them, but to notice when the interview breaks them
2. Prepare 5–7 open questions, not 20 closed ones
3. The best interview question is often: *"Tell me about the last time you [relevant experience]."*

**During: Listen at Three Levels**
- **Words** — what they say
- **Energy** — what they light up about, what they shut down on
- **Absence** — what they don't say, avoid, or can't name

**The Biomimicry Probe Questions**
Alongside standard empathy questions, add:
- *"If this system were a living thing, what kind of organism would it be right now?"*
- *"What would need to change for it to be healthier?"*
- *"What does this system need that it isn't currently getting?"*
- *"Where does this system feel most alive to you?"*

**After: Field Notes, Not Analysis**
Write "I notice…" statements immediately after — not interpretations. Save interpretation for the Pattern stage.

---

### Outputs
- Field notes: "I notice…" observations about each interviewee
- Broken assumptions — list what you expected and what you found instead
- **Energy map** — where in the conversation did they become most alive or most guarded?
- Raw material for your Pattern and Emulate stages

---

### Facilitation Notes
- Silence is a technique. Leave 5 seconds after each answer before speaking.
- Follow the energy — the most important things are often said quietly, after a long pause
- Two interviewers: one asks, one observes body language and takes notes
- Never interview while recording on a device the participant hasn't consented to

---

### Time
**Per interview:** 45–60 min  
**Immediate field notes:** 15 min  
**Synthesis across interviews:** 1–2 hours (after 5+ interviews)

---

### Connected Stages
→ **Observe** (primary — raw material)  
→ **Pattern** (secondary — deepening what recurs)
