# Service Blueprinting
## Root System Study — What Sustains the Canopy?

---

### Nature Analog
A forest's health is visible in its canopy — but determined by its root system. The oldest trees in a forest are connected by mycorrhizal networks: fungi that transfer nutrients between trees, warn of pest attacks, and sustain seedlings that couldn't survive alone. The visible forest is just the above-ground expression of an invisible infrastructure.

A service is the same. What users experience is the canopy. What sustains it — the staff actions, the data systems, the supply chains, the informal care networks — is the root system. Most design focuses on the canopy. Blueprinting maps the roots.

> *"The most important part of the forest is underground."*

---

### Biological Principle
**Below-ground networks.** Suzanne Simard's research on "wood wide web" mycorrhizal networks revealed that trees share resources through fungal connections — the forest is not a collection of competing individuals but a cooperative system. Blueprinting makes that cooperative infrastructure visible.

---

### What It Is
A Service Blueprint is a systems map that places the **user journey at the centre** and maps all the backstage actions, support processes, technology systems, and organisational structures that enable each moment of that journey.

It reveals:
- What is visible to the user (canopy)
- What staff do out of sight (roots)
- What systems operate automatically (mycorrhizal — invisible infrastructure)
- Where the line of **visibility** sits — what users see vs. what they don't

---

### When to Use
- **Prototype phase** — to understand whether your concept is viable at a systems level
- **Propagate phase** — to plan what infrastructure a pilot needs to be sustainable at scale
- When a service keeps failing despite good intentions — the backstage is usually where the failure lives

---

### Biomimetic Method

**The Blueprint Layers**
Map from top to bottom:

| Layer | Nature Analog | What It Contains |
|-------|--------------|-----------------|
| **User journey** | Animal moving through habitat | User actions, touchpoints, moments of experience |
| **Line of visibility** | Water surface | The boundary between what users see and don't |
| **Frontstage actions** | Visible plant growth | Staff actions the user encounters |
| **Backstage actions** | Root growth | Staff actions out of sight |
| **Support processes** | Mycorrhizal network | Systems, technology, supply chains |
| **External actors** | Surrounding ecosystem | Partners, regulators, informal networks |

**Step 1: Walk the Journey**
Start by mapping the full user journey from first contact to last — every touchpoint, every moment of decision or experience. Use your Observe and Prototype stage outputs.

**Step 2: Drop Below the Surface**
For each user touchpoint, ask:
- What must a staff member *visibly do* to enable this?
- What must someone *invisibly do* to enable this?
- What system must *automatically do* to enable this?
- Who from outside the organisation must act?

**Step 3: Find the Root Failures**
Mark where:
- 🔴 **Root breaks** — backstage actions that regularly fail and cascade into frontstage failures
- 🟡 **Invisible load** — backstage labour that is invisible, under-resourced, or carried by informal networks
- 🟢 **Mycelium moments** — where the system is quietly sustaining itself through worker improvisation

**Step 4: Design the Backstage**
Most service blueprints stop at mapping. Push further:
- What needs to *change* in the root system for the canopy to thrive?
- Whose invisible labour needs to become visible and resourced?
- Where does the system need new mycorrhizal connections — support structures that distribute load?

---

### Outputs
- A layered blueprint diagram (physical or digital)
- A list of root failures — backstage conditions causing frontstage pain
- An infrastructure plan for sustaining the service beyond a pilot
- Clarity on what is not yet designed — the invisible systems

---

### Facilitation Notes
- Include frontline staff in the blueprinting session — they know the backstage better than anyone
- Colour-code by actor type — different organisms in the ecosystem
- A blueprint reveals complexity. That is its purpose. Don't simplify it to make it comfortable.

---

### Time
**Preparation:** 45 min  
**Mapping session:** 2–3 hours  
**Analysis and next steps:** 1 hour

---

### Connected Stages
→ **Prototype** (primary — testing whether the concept is systemically viable)  
→ **Propagate** (secondary — planning the infrastructure for scale)
