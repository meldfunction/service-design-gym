# Actor Mapping
## Symbiosis Mapping — Who Feeds Whom, Who Depends on Whom?

---

### Nature Analog
A reef ecosystem cannot be understood by studying fish alone. The coral provides structure; the algae provide food; the cleaner shrimp provide maintenance; the sea urchins provide grazing control. Remove one and the whole system reorganises — sometimes catastrophically. Understanding who depends on whom, and what each actor gives and receives, is the foundation of ecological literacy.

> *"In nature, there are no isolated actors — only nodes in a web of mutual dependency."*
> — Fritjof Capra

---

### Biological Principle
**Trophic networks.** Every organism in an ecosystem occupies a trophic position — it receives energy from below and passes energy upward. Trophic cascades show how removing one species ripples through the entire network. Actor mapping applies the same logic to service systems: who are the producers, consumers, decomposers, and keystone species?

---

### What It Is
Actor Mapping is a structured method for identifying all the humans, organisations, and systems involved in a service ecosystem, and charting the relationships — dependencies, flows, power dynamics, and potential conflicts — between them.

Unlike a stakeholder register (which lists actors), actor mapping reveals **the web of relationships** between them.

---

### When to Use
- **Go-to-Field phase** — to identify who must be aligned before deployment
- **Observe phase** — to understand the full ecosystem before researching
- **Business Design phase** — to map the partner ecosystem for the business model
- Whenever a service crosses organisational boundaries or involves many interdependent actors

---

### Biomimetic Method

**Step 1: Census the Actors**
List every actor who touches the service or is affected by it. Include:
- Direct users (front-of-ecosystem actors)
- Delivery staff and operational actors
- Enabling partners (infrastructure, funding, regulation)
- Edge actors (people affected but not served)
- Invisible actors (processes, algorithms, policies that act without a face)

**Step 2: Map the Flows**
Between each pair of actors, identify:
- **What flows toward them** (information, resources, authority, care)
- **What flows away from them** (value, risk, effort, data)
- **What is blocked or contested** (the friction points)

Use arrows with labels — not just connection lines.

**Step 3: Identify the Trophic Roles**

| Role | Nature Analog | In Service Systems |
|------|--------------|-------------------|
| **Keystone species** | Sea otter, wolf | The actor whose absence collapses the system |
| **Producer** | Plants | Creates the primary value other actors depend on |
| **Consumer** | Herbivore | Receives value directly; drives demand |
| **Decomposer** | Fungi | Processes waste/feedback; enables renewal |
| **Parasite** | Cuckoo | Extracts value without reciprocating |
| **Pioneer** | Lichens | First mover; enables others to follow |

**Step 4: Surface Power Asymmetries**
Highlight:
- Actors who hold disproportionate power without accountability
- Actors who bear disproportionate burden without recognition
- Dependencies that are invisible or unacknowledged

**Step 5: Go-to-Field Implications**
For deployment: which actors must be onboarded? In what order? What does each need to give and receive to say yes?

---

### Outputs
- A visual actor map: nodes + directional flow arrows
- A trophic role register: who is the keystone, who is the decomposer
- A power asymmetry log
- A deployment sequence: actors to onboard in priority order

---

### Facilitation Notes
- Run with a mixed group — different roles in the room see different actors as important
- Invisible actors (policies, algorithms) are often the most powerful — do not skip them
- Power asymmetry conversations are political — name them clearly, do not smooth them over

---

### Time
**Mapping session:** 2 hours  
**Flow annotation:** 45 min  
**Synthesis and deployment sequencing:** 1 hour

---

### Connected Stages
→ **Go-to-Field** (primary — deployment actor alignment)  
→ **Observe** (secondary — ecosystem scoping)  
→ **Business Design** (secondary — partner mapping)
